About Fairouz

Fairouz
THE VOICE OF LEBANON
◆

A voice unlike any other — a homeland's story, sung since the earliest days.

Fairouz, born Nouhad Haddad, is a Lebanese singer widely regarded as one of the greatest voices in the history of Arab music. Her career began in the 1950s, and over decades she built an extraordinary musical legacy that weaves together Lebanese folk tradition, classical melody, and deeply emotional songs.

She collaborated primarily with the Rahbani Brothers, producing musicals and timeless works that became part of the collective Arab memory.

To this day, Fairouz's songs fill quiet mornings and family evenings, continuing to find their way into the hearts of new listeners around the world.

She was born Nouhad Wadih Haddad in Beirut — sources differ on whether it was November 1934 or 1935 — the eldest of four children in a Christian family. Her talent was spotted while she was still in school, and after she joined Radio Lebanon as a chorus singer in the late 1940s, station director Halim El Roumi gave her the stage name "Fairouz," Arabic for turquoise.

At the radio station she met Assi and Mansour Rahbani, the composer brothers who would write most of her songs. Their first hit together, "Itab" ("Blame"), made her a star almost overnight in 1952. She married Assi Rahbani in 1955; the couple had four children — Ziad, Layal, Hali, and Rima.

Fairouz's first major concert was at the Baalbeck International Festival in 1957, which became an annual showcase for her and the Rahbani Brothers until the festival was suspended by the Lebanese Civil War in 1975. Together they created more than twenty musical plays and three feature films.

During the 1975–1990 civil war, Fairouz stayed in Lebanon and refused to take political sides. Her song "Li Beirut" ("To Beirut"), written in 1984, became one of the era's defining anthems of hope.

After the war ended, she returned to the stage in Beirut's Martyrs' Square in 1994 and finally back to Baalbeck in 1998. From the late 1970s on, she also worked closely with her son Ziad, whose jazz-inflected arrangements gave her music a more modern edge.

Over a career spanning more than six decades, Fairouz has recorded close to 1,500 songs and released more than 80 albums, performing at venues from the Paris Olympia to Carnegie Hall.

Famously shy and private, Fairouz rarely gives interviews and has always insisted on singing for the public rather than for individual rulers or private occasions.
